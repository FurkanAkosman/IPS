How Do Environmental Factors Influence the Behavior and Reproduction of Guppies (Poecilia reticulata)?

By Ömer Efe Merey • 02 Nov 2025

Abstract

This article explores how environmental factors—especially light and water conditions—affect the behavior and breeding of guppies (Poecilia reticulata). It combines personal aquarium observations with John A. Endler’s 1987 findings, showing how these fish reflect broader ecological and evolutionary principles.

1. Introduction

Guppies are widely used in aquaria and research for their ease of breeding and sensitivity to environmental change. I am Ömer Efe Merey, a second-year Molecular Biology and Genetics student at Istanbul Sabahattin Zaim University. My interest began with breeding my own guppies and evolved into an academic focus on animal experimentation and behavioral biology. I aim to study genetic, behavioral, and environmental interactions through interdisciplinary projects.

2. Background: Biology of Guppies

  • Origin: South America; warm freshwater habitats.
  • Typical ranges: ~26 °C; pH 6.8–7.6.
  • Livebearers; gestation ≈ 30 days; females can give birth monthly.
  • Gravid spot darkens before birth; fry should be separated due to cannibalism risk.

3. Aquarium Conditions and Maintenance

  • Stocking guideline ≈ 5 L water per guppy; air pump, heater, and filter required.
  • Live plants improve water quality; soil + lava rock aid growth.
  • Supplements: vitamins/conditioners, bacterial cultures, liquid plant fertilizers.
  • Weekly ¼ water change with rested, dechlorinated water.
  • Fry care: separate tank; maturity in ~3–4 months.

4. Scientific Findings: Endler (1987)

Predation and light intensity modulate male courtship. Low light increases visual displays; bright light and/or predators reduce displays and increase “sneak copulation.” This flexibility balances survival and reproduction.

5. Research Focus

Assess how light intensity and water conditions influence breeding and behavior by combining personal observations with literature.

6. Observations and Personal Findings

Activity and courtship were higher in low-to-moderate light, especially in the morning and evening. Bright light increased caution and plant-seeking. Consistent softer lighting correlated with more fry.

7. Discussion

Guppies show context-dependent adaptation to light, temperature, and risk. Even in controlled aquaria, selection pressures and ecological balance are observable.

8. Conclusion

Balanced lighting, stable temperature and chemistry, low waste load, and structural enrichment improve behavioral welfare and reproductive success.
